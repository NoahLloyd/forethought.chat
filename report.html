End-to-end LLM judging is intentionally not used for citations: the "real paper, but doesn't actually support the claim" failure is invisible to end-to-end judging and the most damaging trust failure for a research-grounded agent.

Question. In Forethought's 'Three Types of Intelligence Explosion' (Davidson, Hadshar, MacAskill), what probability do the authors give to the software feedback loop alone being sufficient to sustain accelerating progress in a software intelligence explosion?

Target. around 50%

Agent answer (1473 chars) 
The answer is clearly stated in the paper. According to *Three Types of Intelligence Explosion* (Davidson, Hadshar, MacAskill, 2025-03-17), the authors assign **~50% probability** to the software feedback loop alone being sufficient to sustain accelerating progress.

Here is the exact language from the piece:

> "A software IE might well accelerate over time, because the software feedback loop *by itself* might well be enough to sustain accelerating progress (~50% likely)." [6]

The authors ground this estimate in empirical data on AI efficiency gains — specifically, Epoch research estimating that doubling research inputs leads to roughly 0.8 to 3.5 doublings of compute efficiency across several domains — as well as their own modelling, which estimates approximately 1.2 doublings of output for every doubling of cognitive input (range: 0.4 to 3.6) [6].

For comparison, the paper places the software-only case as the most uncertain of the three feedback loops they analyse. The chip *technology* loop alone is rated at ~65%, and the chip *production* loop alone at ~80%. When all loops are combined in a full-stack intelligence explosion, the probability of accelerating progress rises to ~90% [1, 2].

So the ~50% figure reflects genuine uncertainty: the authors think a software intelligence explosion *accelerating* is roughly a coin-flip, not a near-certainty — and that confidence grows substantially once hardware feedback loops are added into the picture.

Question. According to Forethought's 'How quick and big would a software intelligence explosion be?' paper, what probability do the authors assign to the software intelligence explosion compressing more than 3 years of AI progress into less than 1 year?

Target. around 60%

Agent answer (1892 chars) 
The answer is clearly stated in the paper. According to Davidson and Houlden's *How quick and big would a software intelligence explosion be?* (2025-08-05), the authors assign a **~60% probability** to the software intelligence explosion compressing more than 3 years of AI progress into less than 1 year [1].

They pair this with a second, more extreme threshold: they consider it **only ~20% likely** that the explosion would compress more than 10 years of progress into less than 1 year [1]. The abstract of the paper captures the intuition neatly: "AI progress could easily be 3X faster, might be 10X faster, but won't be 30X faster — because at that speed we'd quickly hit limits on how good software can get" [7].

Importantly, these figures refer specifically to *software*-driven progress — that is, progress achieved solely through algorithmic improvements, holding compute constant. The authors note that if compute continues to scale during this period (as they consider likely), that would drive *additional* progress on top [1, 2].

Their overall framing is deliberately intermediate: the analysis pushes back against both hard sceptics of intelligence explosion dynamics and those who anticipate extremely rapid, sustained capability jumps, landing instead on "a significant additional acceleration at just the time when AI systems are surpassing top humans in broad areas of science and engineering" [1].

If you're interested in digging deeper, you might want to look at how they handle the question of what slows or stops the explosion — the companion pieces on [compute bottlenecks](https://www.forethought.org/research/will-compute-bottlenecks-prevent-a-software-intelligence-explosion) and [retraining costs](https://www.forethought.org/research/will-the-need-to-retrain-ai-models) are directly relevant to why the upper tail (>10 years compressed) is considered unlikely.

Agent answer (731 chars) 
The answer is right there in the paper. The authors conclude that the arguments "fairly robustly point towards [trillion-year stable societies] being at least plausibly possible," and that their feasibility is worth **at least 20% subjective probability** [1].
