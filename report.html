End-to-end LLM judging is intentionally not used for citations: the "real paper, but doesn't actually support the claim" failure is invisible to end-to-end judging and the most damaging trust failure for a research-grounded agent.

Question. In Forethought's 'Three Types of Intelligence Explosion' (Davidson, Hadshar, MacAskill), what probability do the authors give to the software feedback loop alone being sufficient to sustain accelerating progress in a software intelligence explosion?

Target. around 50%

Agent answer (1581 chars) 
The answer is clear and directly stated in the paper. Davidson, Hadshar, and MacAskill give the software feedback loop alone a **~50% probability** of being sufficient to sustain accelerating progress in a software intelligence explosion [6].

To put that figure in context, the paper compares it to the other feedback loops, which the authors consider progressively stronger [6]:

- **Software feedback loop alone**: ~50% likely to sustain accelerating progress ("might well be enough")
- **Chip technology feedback loop alone**: ~65% ("probably enough")
- **Chip production feedback loop alone**: ~80% ("very likely enough")
- **Software + chip technology combined** (AI-technology IE): ~75%
- **All three combined** (full-stack IE): ~90% ("highly likely to accelerate")

The reasoning behind the 50% figure draws on empirical estimates of returns to software R&D. Epoch research suggests that doubling research inputs yields somewhere between 0.8 and 3.5 doublings of compute efficiency across domains, and Davidson and Houlden's own modelling estimates roughly 1.2 doublings of output per doubling of cognitive input — but with a wide range (0.4 to 3.6), which explains the authors' epistemic caution and the roughly coin-flip probability [6].

So the software-only case is genuinely uncertain: the authors think it is plausible but by no means assured, and the ~50% figure reflects the wide spread in empirical estimates of whether the feedback loop is strong enough (i.e., whether returns to software R&D exceed 1) to keep progress accelerating rather than plateauing [2, 6].

Question. According to Forethought's 'How quick and big would a software intelligence explosion be?' paper, what probability do the authors assign to the software intelligence explosion compressing more than 3 years of AI progress into less than 1 year?

Target. around 60%

Agent answer (1191 chars) 
The answer is clearly stated in the paper. According to Davidson and Houlden's *How quick and big would a software intelligence explosion be?* (2025-08-05), the authors assign a probability of approximately **~60%** to the software intelligence explosion compressing more than 3 years of AI progress into less than 1 year [1].

For context, they pair this with a second estimate: they consider it only **~20% likely** that the explosion compresses *more than 10 years* of progress into less than 1 year [1]. The paper's framing is explicitly an intermediate view — rejecting both deep scepticism about intelligence explosion dynamics and expectations of an extremely rapid, unbounded acceleration. As they put it, AI progress during a software intelligence explosion "could easily be 3X faster, might be 10X faster, but won't be 30X faster — because at that speed we'd quickly hit limits on how good software can get" [7].

Importantly, these figures refer to progress from *software improvements alone*, with compute held constant. The authors note that if compute is also still increasing during this period — which they consider likely — that would drive *additional* progress on top [1].

Question. In Forethought's 'AGI and Lock-in' paper (Lukas Finnveden), what subjective probability do the authors say the feasibility of a trillion-year stable society is worth, at minimum?

Target. at least 20%

Agent answer (964 chars) 
The answer is right there in the paper. The authors — Finnveden, Riedel, and Shulman — conclude that the arguments "fairly robustly point towards [trillion-year stable societies] being at least plausibly possible," and specifically that **their feasibility is worth at least 20% subjective probability** [1].

To give that figure context: they note they are especially confident that a civilization stable for *millions* of years is possible (since that is compatible with complete stagnation), while stability over *billions or trillions* of years introduces additional challenges — including potential encounters with alien civilizations, the need for space travel, and unpredictable physical events over cosmic timescales. Of those challenges, alien civilizations strike them as the most likely threat to an otherwise locked-in scenario. Setting that aside, they say it seems **more likely than not** that a trillion-year stable society is possible overall [1].
